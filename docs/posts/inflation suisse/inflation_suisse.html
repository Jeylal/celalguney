Inflation and Price Level in Switzerland

Introduction

In the last two decades, inflation has remained remarkably low in Switzerland. From 2000 to 2025, the national Swiss consumer price index (CPI) has remained nearly constant, with an average annual growth rate of 0,6%. In international comparison, only Japan has experienced lower inflation (0,5%), while average inflation rates ranged from 1,5 to 2,5% in developed economies (for instance 2% for the Eurozone and 2,5% for the United States). What accounts for Switzerland’s remarkable and enduring resilience to inflationary pressures? The literature has advanced several competing hypotheses, each emphasising different structural, institutional, or macroeconomic mechanisms. The most prominent of these are examined in turn below.

The Safe-Haven Currency Hypothesis

The Swiss franc is widely regarded as a safe-haven currency, serving as a global store of value during periods of economic and geopolitical uncertainty. Its status as a refuge currency dates back to at least the First World War, when Switzerland’s comparative monetary stability stood in sharp contrast to the widespread currency upheaval that followed the Great War. Switzerland’s non-belligerence contributed substantially to the franc’s attractiveness, a status further consolidated by the stability of Swiss socio-political institutions. While the franc’s safe-haven role was reinforced progressively throughout the post-war period, it became fully manifest with the collapse of the Bretton Woods system in 1973: the dissolution of the dollar peg triggered a sustained and substantial nominal appreciation of the Swiss franc. In theory, this secular appreciation should exert downward pressure on inflation by lowering the relative price of imported goods and services. However, the empirical literature has called this mechanism into question by documenting a strikingly incomplete exchange rate pass-through. R. Auer, Burstein, and Lein (2021) find that a 1% appreciation of the Swiss franc translates into a decline in retail import prices of only 0.55% after two quarters. Oktay (2022) estimates an overall pass-through coefficient of 0.12 across the full CPI basket, implying that only 12% of an EUR/CHF exchange rate shock is transmitted to Swiss consumer prices. The pass-through is thus substantially incomplete, which considerably weakens the case for currency appreciation as a primary driver of Switzerland’s low inflation performance.

The Administered Prices Hypothesis

A second line of explanation points to the comparatively large share of administered prices within Switzerland’s HICP basket. Switzerland exhibits one of the highest proportions of fully or partially regulated prices among European economies. In the wake of the supply shocks triggered by the COVID-19 pandemic and the Russian invasion of Ukraine, post-Keynesian economists — most prominently Isabella Weber — argued in favour of price controls as an instrument to combat cost-push inflation, particularly when amplified by sellers’ inflation dynamics in which firms exploit crisis conditions to expand profit margins (Weber and Wasner 2023). On this reading, the prevalence of administered prices in Switzerland constitutes a structural mechanism that dampens inflationary pressures by insulating a significant portion of the consumption basket from market price volatility.

However, recent empirical evidence suggests that administered prices are not the primary driver of Switzerland’s low inflation performance. The vast majority of Swiss administered prices correspond to healthcare goods and services, which are classified as non-administered in several euro area countries, or attributed to the public sector rather than private consumption on account of their public funding. Crucially, not only have administered prices risen less in Switzerland than in the euro area, but so have non-administered prices. S. Auer, Beyeler, and Meier (2025) show that administered prices account for less than one quarter of the observed inflation differential between Switzerland and the euro area. The dominant share of the differential is therefore explained by subdued price dynamics in the non-administered segment, pointing to deeper structural factors at work.

The CPI Construction Hypothesis

A third hypothesis holds that Switzerland’s low measured inflation is in part a statistical artefact, reflecting the specific structure and expenditure weights of the Swiss CPI basket. Critics point, in particular, to the relatively low weight assigned to energy and to the exclusion of certain major expenditure items — most notably compulsory health insurance premiums — from the standard consumer price index. Some economists have addressed this by re-estimating Swiss inflation using the expenditure weights applied in other countries, such as Germany. Nevertheless, even under these counterfactual weighting schemes, Swiss inflation remains substantially lower than in comparable economies, suggesting that measurement conventions account for only a modest fraction of the observed differential.

The Wage-Price Spiral and Sellers’ Inflation Hypothesis

A fourth explanation focuses on the limited role played by domestic wage-price dynamics in Switzerland. Given the strongly export-oriented character of Swiss production, increases in unit labour costs tend to be reflected primarily in export prices rather than in domestic consumer prices. Moreover, approximately 40% of the Swiss CPI basket — notably rents and healthcare — lies outside the typical scope of wage-price spiral mechanisms. These authors conclude that Swiss CPI dynamics are driven predominantly by fluctuations in import prices — themselves shaped by movements in oil prices, foreign inflation, the exchange rate, and inflation expectations — rather than by domestic wage-setting processes. This finding is broadly consistent with the sellers’ inflation framework developed by Weber and Wasner (2023): while the data do not support a significant spiral between wages and domestic consumer prices, the question of whether Swiss firms have exploited supply-side disruptions to expand profit margins warrants closer empirical scrutiny.

The Energy Mix Hypothesis

Fluctuations in oil and natural gas prices rank among the most powerful drivers of consumer price inflation. As fossil fuels constitute a critical intermediate input in the production of a wide range of goods and services, energy price shocks propagate rapidly through value chains and into the broader price level. The supply disruptions triggered by the COVID-19 pandemic and, more forcefully, by the Russian invasion of Ukraine, were instrumental in the resurgence of inflation across Europe from 2021 onwards.

Switzerland’s comparatively limited dependence on fossil fuels — owing to its substantial reliance on hydroelectric and nuclear power generation — implies that energy price shocks transmit less forcefully to domestic consumer prices than in more fossil-fuel-dependent economies. This structural characteristic provides a natural buffer against the kind of cost-push dynamics that drove the post-pandemic inflation episode across much of the continent.

While this explanation is intuitively compelling, it is complicated by the observation that several countries with even greener energy mixes, Iceland, Sweden, Norway, and Finland, nonetheless experienced higher inflation rates than Switzerland. The relevant distinguishing factor appears to be institutional rather than merely technical: Switzerland operates a regulated public energy market that anchors electricity and energy retail prices to domestic production costs. By contrast, the Nordic countries, despite their predominantly renewable generation capacity, are integrated into the European internal energy market, where prices are set at the marginal cost of the most expensive dispatched source, typically imported gas or coal. Their generation is low-carbon, but the price of that energy is nonetheless aligned with fossil fuel benchmarks. Switzerland’s institutional insulation from this pricing mechanism provides an additional anti-inflationary buffer that a green energy mix alone cannot replicate.

A further dimension of this hypothesis concerns energy intensity. Switzerland ranks as the advanced economy with the lowest energy intensity of production, consuming exceptionally little energy per unit of gross value added. Low energy intensity implies that supply-side price shocks translate less directly into consumer price inflation and propagate less extensively through domestic value chains. A recent study by the State Secretariat for Economic Affairs (SECO) confirms that this efficiency advantage is pervasive across all sectors of the Swiss economy, agriculture, industry, services, and households alike. Swiss manufacturing, for instance, consumes less than one-third of the energy used in French manufacturing and less than half of that used in German manufacturing per unit of gross value added. This structural advantage has been further reinforced since 2010 by the expansion of the pharmaceutical sector, which is characterised by relatively low energy intensity and whose share of Swiss manufacturing value added rose from 15% to 31% between 2010 and 2019.
